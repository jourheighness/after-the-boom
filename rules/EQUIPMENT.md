# MONDAS Equipment Catalog v0.1

Everything on this list exists in 1992 Mondas. Weapons use the dice categories from `rules/CORE.md`. Properties (Ranged, Sidearm, Thrown, Long, Loud, Brutal, Subtle, Slow) are defined there. Thaumatech items marked [Contact plate] require a Positive operator. Armor values are flat damage reduction (0-3).

## Make Your Own

The catalog above covers common gear. Your character carries what makes sense for their life. Here's how to build something that isn't listed.

### Weapons

Pick a damage die based on size and lethality:

| Category | Die | Examples |
|-|-|-|
| Unarmed / Improvised | 1d4 | Fists, bottles, rocks, a telephone receiver |
| Light | 1d6 | Knives, batons, hatchets, saps |
| Medium | 1d8 | Machetes, baseball bats, crowbars, handguns |
| Heavy | 2d6 | Shotguns, sledgehammers, rifles |
| Devastating | 2d8 | Military weapons, vehicle-mounted, extremely rare |

Then add properties that fit. Properties are not bonuses. They describe how the weapon works and what it costs you.

- **Ranged:** Can hit targets at Near or Far. Most firearms.
- **Sidearm:** Can be concealed on your person. Small enough to hide.
- **Thrown:** Can be thrown at Near range. You don't have it anymore after.
- **Long:** Reach advantage at Close. Disadvantage in tight spaces.
- **Loud:** Everyone nearby hears it. Attracts attention.
- **Brutal:** Extra messy. Consequences hit harder.
- **Subtle:** Quiet. Easy to miss.
- **Slow:** Takes time between uses. Bolt-action rifles, crossbows.

**The rule:** more properties means more interesting, not more powerful. A weapon with Loud and Brutal is dramatic and attention-grabbing, not strictly better than one with Subtle.

### Armor

Armor provides a flat damage reduction (Armor 1, 2, or 3) but always costs something:

- **Armor 1:** Light protection. A leather jacket, a mail-order vest. Minimal penalty.
- **Armor 2:** Serious protection. Military surplus, thaumic weave. Usually imposes Snag on Sharp (perception) or similar.
- **Armor 3:** Maximum available protection. Rare, military-grade. Always has significant penalties.

If you want armor that doesn't exist in the catalog, pick the Armor value and name the penalty. The GM confirms.

### Thaumatech and Gear

For thaumatech devices, describe what it does in one sentence, how the contact plate works, and assign an **Overcharge stat** (Sharp for precision/detection, Nerve for channeling/control/medical, Grit for brute-force/physical). The GM sets the mechanical effect. All thaumatech requires Positive status (contact plate activation).

For mundane gear, describe what it does and what Boon it provides. One item, one benefit. A climbing harness gives Boon on climbing rolls. It doesn't also give Boon on falling, stealth, and intimidation.

### Gambits for Custom Gear

When you create a weapon, give it 1-3 Gambits. Ask:

- **What's this weapon good at besides hitting people?** A hook drags. A club stuns. A rifle controls space. That's your first Gambit.
- **What's the weapon's personality?** A quiet weapon gets subtle Gambits. A loud weapon gets dramatic ones. A brutal weapon gets messy ones.
- **What would make a player sacrifice damage for this?** If the answer is "nothing, I'd just keep the damage," the Gambit isn't interesting enough.

Format: name, one sentence, range. Standard (4+) by default. Mark (Strong) if the effect is powerful enough to warrant an 8+.

For non-weapon gear, ask: "Does this item do something interesting in a fight or a tense situation beyond its base function?" If yes, give it one Gambit. If no, skip it.

### Talk to the GM

"My character would carry [item]. I think it should work like [category/die] with [properties] because [reason]."

The GM might adjust the die, add a property, or note a consequence. That's the system working. A homemade weapon might be fragile. A modified firearm might be illegal. The interesting part is what your gear says about you.

### The One-Line Test

If you can't describe your item in one line with a die and 0-2 properties, it's too complicated. Simplify until it fits. The fiction handles the rest.
