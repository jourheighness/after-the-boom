# Stakes Gambit Design

## Problem

Players freeze on stakes rolls when told "sacrifice a die, name a thing." The open negotiation has no structure — players don't know what's on the table.

Combat gambits solve this with fixed verbs. Stakes rolls are too fiction-variable for fixed verbs. We need structure that prompts without constraining.

## Design

### Player Rule

After you roll, sacrifice a die showing 4+ to grab something extra from the moment. Sacrifice a 6 for something big. Name it in one sentence. It adds — it never erases.

### Prompt Table

| You're... | You could gambit to... |
|-|-|
| **Forcing something** | create cover, block a path, control the approach, break something free |
| **Talking to someone** | read a tell, plant an idea, establish a reputation, create an alibi |
| **Reading the situation** | spot a detail, sense a shift, connect two facts, anticipate what's next |
| **Working an angle** | lift something, plant something, leave a false trail, time a pattern |

### GM Guidance

Size the gambit to the sacrifice and the character. Spare die = small. Best die = big. In their wheelhouse = generous. A stretch = narrow. The consequence still stands — the gambit adds a fact beside it.

### GM Examples

**Brian (Grit 3) forces a door. Rolls 6, 4, 2. Sacrifices the 4.**
"I brace in the doorway." GM: full cover position. Spare die, in his lane — give generously.

**Murph (Nerve 4) bluffs a checkpoint. Rolls 5, 4, 3, 1. Sacrifices the 4.**
"I clock the shift schedule on the clipboard." GM: next rotation is in 20 minutes. Spare die, bit of a stretch — give it, keep it simple.

**Max (Sharp 2) tails a target. Rolls 5, 4. Sacrifices the 5.**
"I spot his concealed badge." GM: DPA field agent, full ID. Best die, right in his lane — he paid big, give big.

**Oliver (Nerve 3) calms a crowd. Rolls 4, 4, 2. Sacrifices a 4.**
"I notice who in the crowd ISN'T panicking." GM: one face, no details yet. Spare die, stretch into perception — keep it thin.

## Key Constraints

1. **Side effect, not re-attempt.** The gambit produces something new. It does not upgrade the main action's result or cancel a consequence.
2. **Fiction gates everything.** No fiction, no gambit. The GM must see how the side effect flows from the action.
3. **Strong gambit = sacrifice a 6.** Same threshold as combat. On d6 pools, only a natural 6 qualifies. This IS the crit — a choice, not a gift.

## Sizing Principle

The GM doesn't need a matrix. Two axes, applied intuitively:

- **Cost axis:** What did the player give up? A spare die they weren't using, or their best result? Match the effect to the sacrifice.
- **Character axis:** Is this in the character's wheelhouse, or a stretch? Generous for home turf. Narrow for a reach.

These compound naturally. Spare die + stretch = thin. Best die + in-lane = major. The examples teach this better than rules text.

## Niche Protection

No mechanical gating. Any character can attempt any gambit. Protection comes from:

- **Dice math.** Low-stat characters have fewer spare dice and their 4+ die is more likely to be their highest. The cost is naturally higher.
- **GM sizing.** In their wheelhouse = generous, stretch = narrow. A Grit character reading social cues gets a surface-level read. A Nerve character gets layers.
- **Fiction gating.** The GM must see how the fiction supports it. Harder to justify out-of-lane effects.

The specialist gets better deals, not exclusive access.

## Multiple Gambits

A character with multiple spare 4+ dice can sacrifice more than one. Each requires a separate fictional side effect. The fiction must support each independently.

## Relationship to Existing Systems

| Category | Gate | Verbs | Effect sizing |
|-|-|-|-|
| **Combat Gambits** | Battlefield fiction | 7 fixed verbs + open clause | Fixed outputs from combat vocabulary |
| **Shaping Gambits** | Must be a shaper, costs Guard | 8 fixed verbs | Fixed outputs |
| **Stakes Gambits** | Fiction + GM approval | Open — player names it | GM sizes to sacrifice + character |

Stakes Gambits fill the gap between combat's fixed structure and the old "name a thing" open negotiation. They add enough prompt to break the freeze without constraining the fiction.

## Design Journey

Explored and rejected:
- **Stat-gated gambit categories** (Grit/Nerve/Sharp each get their own verbs). Failed: action-gambit overlap. "Persuade on a Nerve roll" is re-attempting, not side-effecting.
- **Outcome-typed categories** (Gain/Shift/Soften). Better structure, but categories collapsed — a narrative fact deployed at the right moment IS consequence mitigation.
- **Crossover mechanics** (extra die cost for out-of-lane). Too punishing — players never cross over, so three categories collapse to one.
- **Two outcome types** (Gain vs Soften). Collapsed — "I have soft shoes" as a Gain negates the consequence, making it functionally Soften.

The prompt table + sizing principle emerged as the minimal viable structure. Categories resist classification because fiction is too fluid. The fix for "players freeze" is a creativity prompt, not a mechanical taxonomy.

## Research References

Design informed by analysis of:
- **Blades in the Dark** — Devil's Bargain: visible trade-off, calibrated to be a real decision
- **Fate Core** — Create Advantage: named narrative facts that persist and stack across team
- **PbtA (7-9 lists)** — Structured choices on partial success; player picks, not GM
- **Dogs in the Vineyard** — Raise/See: three response types (deflect/reverse/take the blow); accepting damage is tactical
- **Burning Wheel** — Artha: gambit should feel like character choice, not player optimization
- **Blades Deep Cuts (2024)** — Threat Roll: success assumed, consequences declared before rolling
